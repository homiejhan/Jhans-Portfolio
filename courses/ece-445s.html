Every time you stream music, send a text, or make a phone call, digital signal processing is happening invisibly beneath the surface. The algorithms that compress your audio, route your Wi-Fi packets, and decode your cellular signal were all designed by engineers who understood how to translate mathematical theory into working code running on constrained hardware — in real time, with no room for error.

That is exactly what this course is about. ECE 445S takes you from the abstract mathematics of signals and systems all the way to a running embedded implementation on an ARM microcontroller. You will design filters, build communication transceivers, and write real-time C code — and each step is motivated by a concrete application: audio effects, wireless modems, biomedical sensors, and radar systems. The goal is not just to understand DSP theory, but to develop the instinct to turn it into something that actually works in the physical world.

By the end, the gap between a textbook equation and a real system closes. When you see a Wi-Fi signal diagram or a digital audio pipeline, you will understand every block — and know how to build it.

Topic 1 Sinusoidal Generation
›

Why This Matters

Before a single bit of data can travel wirelessly between your phone and a cell tower, it must be converted into a sinusoidal wave — a carrier signal oscillating millions of times per second. Your phone generates these carriers digitally, using exactly the algorithms covered here, running on a processor not unlike the STM32 in lab. Understanding how to generate and manipulate sinusoids efficiently is the entry point into every communication and audio system ever built.

This topic also introduces the core design tension of real-time embedded DSP: accuracy vs. speed. The most mathematically correct method for generating a cosine is too slow for real-time use; the fastest method is unstable. Finding the sweet spot — and knowing why it exists — is the kind of engineering judgment this course is designed to build.

Concepts

Every signal in DSP can be decomposed into sinusoids, making them the foundational object of the entire field. A continuous-time cosine is parameterized by its amplitude \(A\), frequency \(f_0\), and phase \(\phi\):

\[ x(t) = A\cos(2\pi f_0 t + \phi) \]

When we sample this signal at rate \(f_s\), we get a discrete-time sequence indexed by integer \(n\), where the continuous frequency \(f_0\) maps to a normalized angular frequency \(\omega_0\):

\[ x[n] = A\cos(\omega_0 n + \phi), \quad \omega_0 = \frac{2\pi f_0}{f_s} \]

One subtlety here is discrete-time periodicity. Unlike continuous time, a discrete-time cosine \(x[n]\) is only periodic when \(f_0/f_s\) is a rational number — if it is irrational, the sequence never exactly repeats. When it is rational, the period is \(N = f_s / \gcd(f_0, f_s)\) samples.

Generating these sinusoids efficiently in real time is a core challenge. There are three main approaches. The direct formula simply evaluates \(\cos(\omega_0 n)\) each sample — maximally accurate but slow due to the cost of the math library call. The recursive LTI oscillator exploits a trigonometric identity to give a two-tap recurrence:

\[ y[n] = 2\cos(\omega_0)\,y[n-1] - y[n-2] \]

This is extremely fast — just two multiplications and two additions per sample — but it is BIBO unstable. Because the poles sit exactly on the unit circle, rounding errors accumulate over time and the output eventually diverges. The lookup table method precomputes one full period of the cosine and uses a phase accumulator (a running counter incremented by \(\omega_0\) each sample) to index into it. This is the best practical tradeoff: fast, memory-efficient, and numerically stable.

Beyond generation, sinusoids are the mechanism for modulation: multiplying a baseband signal by a carrier cosine shifts its spectrum to a higher frequency (upconversion), while multiplying by another cosine at the receiver shifts it back (downconversion). This is the principle behind AM/FM radio, Wi-Fi, and cellular systems. The bandwidth of any real signal is bounded below by the thermal noise floor and above by the system's modulation bandwidth — ranging from ~10 kHz for AM radio to 400 MHz for 5G mmWave.

Key Takeaways

  • The recursive LTI oscillator method is computationally cheap but is BIBO unstable — numerical errors accumulate over time
  • Sampling a cosine at exactly its frequency yields ambiguous results; sampling rate must exceed twice the signal frequency (Nyquist)
  • Tremolo (rapid volume variation) and vibrato (rapid pitch variation) are both modulation effects — connecting theory to audio
  • Finite observation of any signal broadens its frequency-domain representation

Applications

Sinusoidal generation underlies every communication system front end. AM/FM radio, Wi-Fi upconversion/downconversion, and audio effects like tremolo all rely on sinusoidal modulation. The mixer circuit studied here reappears in the QAM transmitter and receiver later in the course.

Topic 2 Discrete-Time Signals and Systems
›

Why This Matters

Every filter you will build — from the lowpass audio filter in Lab 3 to the matched filter in Lab 5 — is a Linear Time-Invariant system. LTI theory is not an abstraction for its own sake; it is the reason filter design is even possible. Because LTI systems can only scale and delay frequencies that already exist, a designer can predict exactly what a filter will do to any input just by knowing its frequency response. No simulation needed — the math fully characterizes the behavior.

The spectrogram, introduced here, becomes your primary diagnostic tool for the rest of the semester. When something in a lab goes wrong, the spectrogram tells you where the problem is in frequency and time.

Concepts

Before designing filters, we need a rigorous language for talking about signals and the systems that transform them. Signals come in many flavors — the unit step, Dirac delta \(\delta(t)\), rectangular pulse, and impulse train — each useful for different kinds of analysis. The Dirac delta is especially important: it is the identity element for convolution, meaning convolving any signal with \(\delta(t)\) returns the signal unchanged.

Systems are characterized by two key properties. Linearity means the system satisfies superposition: scaling or adding inputs produces the same change in the output. Time-invariance means the system's behavior does not change with time: a delayed input produces a delayed (but otherwise identical) output. When both properties hold simultaneously, we have a Linear Time-Invariant (LTI) system, and the input-output relationship is fully described by convolution with the system's impulse response \(h[n]\):

The critical insight is that LTI systems do not create new frequencies — they can only scale and phase-shift frequency components that already exist in the input. This is why they are so useful as filters. In the frequency domain, convolution in time becomes multiplication, and the system's frequency response \(H(e^{j\omega})\) tells us exactly how each frequency is scaled and shifted.

Discrete-time frequency \(\omega\) (in radians per sample) relates to physical frequency \(f\) (in Hz) through the sampling rate:

\[ \omega = \frac{2\pi f}{f_s} \quad\text{(rad/sample)} \]

This means the highest representable frequency — the Nyquist frequency at \(\omega = \pi\) rad/sample — corresponds to \(f_s/2\) Hz. For audio systems running at 48 kHz, that is 24 kHz, comfortably above the human hearing range of 20 Hz to ~20 kHz. One useful tool for visualizing signals is the spectrogram, which computes short-time FFTs to show how frequency content evolves over time — used throughout the lab to analyze the effect of filters on real audio.

A concrete LTI example is the averaging FIR filter, which outputs the running mean of its last \(N\) inputs. It is a lowpass filter — it smooths out rapid fluctuations. Its group delay is constant at \((N-1)/2\) samples, meaning it delays all frequencies equally. The ideal lowpass filter impulse response is the sinc function:

\[ \text{sinc}(x) = \frac{\sin(\pi x)}{\pi x} \]

It is infinite in duration and non-causal, so in practice it must be truncated and windowed — but it sets the theoretical target that all practical filter designs aim toward.

Key Takeaways

  • Linearity and time-invariance must both hold for a system to be LTI — amplitude modulation is time-varying and thus not LTI
  • Initial conditions matter: an integrator under observation starting at t ≥ 0 may not satisfy LTI properties even if it would for all time
  • The ideal lowpass filter impulse response is the sinc function — it is derived in the Concepts section above, and it sets the theoretical target all practical filter designs aim toward
  • Weighted SNR (WSNR) incorporates application-specific perceptual weighting into the SNR measure

Applications

LTI theory is the mathematical backbone of every filter in this course. Understanding convolution and frequency response is prerequisite for designing FIR and IIR filters, and the spectrogram tool is used throughout the lab to visualize what filters are actually doing to real audio signals.

Topic 3 FIR Filters
›

Why This Matters

FIR filters are everywhere: the anti-aliasing filter in your phone's ADC, the equalization stage in a digital audio workstation, the matched filter in a 4G modem, the edge-detection kernel in a camera's image processor. They are the most widely deployed class of digital filter because their properties — guaranteed stability, linear phase, and exact analysis — make them safe and predictable to deploy in products where failure is not an option.

In this course, FIR filters appear in Labs 2, 3, 5, and 6. Understanding them deeply is not optional — it is the prerequisite for everything that follows.

Concepts

An FIR filter computes its output as a weighted sum of the current and past \(N-1\) inputs, where \(h[k]\) are the filter coefficients (its impulse response):

\[ y[n] = \sum_{k=0}^{N-1} h[k]\,x[n-k] \]

This requires exactly \(N\) multiplications and \(N-1\) additions per output sample — a fixed, predictable cost that maps perfectly to the multiply-accumulate hardware on modern DSP processors and microcontrollers. Because there is no feedback, the output depends only on a finite window of past inputs, guaranteeing unconditional BIBO stability regardless of coefficient values.

To analyze a filter's behavior in the frequency domain, we use the Z-transform. The transfer function \(H(z)\) is a polynomial in \(z^{-1}\), and the frequency response is obtained by evaluating it on the unit circle:

\[ H(e^{j\omega}) = H(z)\big|_{z=e^{j\omega}} \]

The magnitude \(|H(e^{j\omega})|\) tells us how each frequency is amplified or attenuated, while the phase \(\angle H(e^{j\omega})\) tells us the time delay applied to that frequency. A critical design goal in audio and communications is linear phase — where all frequencies experience the same delay. This is guaranteed whenever the impulse response is symmetric: \(h[n] = h[N-1-n]\). Under this condition, the phase is exactly linear:

\[ \angle H(e^{j\omega}) = -\omega\,\frac{N-1}{2} \]

This means the constant group delay is \(\tau = (N-1)/2\) samples — a half-sample delay for odd-length filters. All frequencies arrive at the output with the same time offset, which preserves the shape of transient signals like speech and prevents the smearing that nonlinear phase would cause.

One practical concern in fixed-point implementations is wordlength expansion: summing \(N\) products of \(B\)-bit numbers requires more than \(B\) bits to represent exactly. The output of an \(N\)-tap FIR filter needs roughly \(\log_2(N)\) extra bits — something the C implementation on the STM32 must handle through careful data-type selection. Cascading two FIR filters is straightforward — the combined transfer function is simply the product of the individual transfer functions — making it easy to build complex responses from simpler stages.

Key Takeaways

  • A symmetric FIR impulse response guarantees linear phase (constant group delay) — critical for audio and communications applications
  • Time delay in the time domain corresponds to a linear phase shift in the frequency domain
  • LTI systems convolve in the time domain and multiply in the frequency domain — fundamental theorem of LTI systems
  • Removing the phase of an image's FFT (setting it to zero) destroys the structural content while keeping the magnitude — phase matters more than magnitude for images

Applications

FIR filters are implemented on the STM32 in Lab 3 to filter audio in real time. Surface acoustic wave (SAW) filters used in cell phones are physical realizations of FIR filters. FIR equalization is used in Lab 5 to compensate for channel distortion in PAM communication systems.

Topic 4 IIR Filters
›

Why This Matters

Achieving a sharp frequency cutoff with an FIR filter requires hundreds of taps and the corresponding computation. IIR filters achieve the same selectivity with a fraction of the multiplications — which is why they dominate in hardware-constrained applications: hearing aids, automotive ECUs, EEG/ECG monitors, and telephone networks all rely on IIR filtering for real-time operation on low-power processors. The tradeoff is stability and phase linearity: understanding when each approach is appropriate is a core professional skill for any DSP engineer.

Concepts

While FIR filters are unconditionally stable and easy to design, they can require hundreds of taps to achieve sharp frequency selectivity. IIR filters solve this by introducing feedback — the output at time \(n\) is fed back into the computation at future samples. This recursion gives the filter an "infinite memory," allowing it to achieve very sharp roll-off with just a handful of coefficients. The simplest IIR structure is the first-order all-pole filter:

\[ H(z) = \frac{1}{1 - az^{-1}}, \quad \text{pole at } z = a \]

The coefficient \(a\) controls both the frequency of the resonance and the stability. BIBO stability requires that the pole lies strictly inside the unit circle in the z-plane. The general stability condition for any IIR filter is:

\[ |p_k| < 1 \quad \forall\, k \]

where \(p_k\) are all the poles of \(H(z)\). When a pole sits close to the unit circle at angle \(\theta\), the filter exhibits a sharp resonance near frequency \(\omega = \theta\) rad/sample — this is the fundamental mechanism for frequency selectivity. A zero at the same angle creates an exact null (notch) in the magnitude response.

In practice, all IIR filters of order greater than two are implemented as cascades of biquad sections (second-order sections). A single biquad has the general form:

\[ H(z) = \frac{b_0 + b_1 z^{-1} + b_2 z^{-2}}{1 + a_1 z^{-1} + a_2 z^{-2}} \]

This is the universal IIR building block. By combining biquads in cascade, we can realize any higher-order filter while keeping each section numerically well-conditioned — essential because expanding a factored high-order polynomial into a single set of coefficients causes severe coefficient sensitivity (a small perturbation to one coefficient shifts the pole locations dramatically). The all-pass filter is a special IIR case with \(|H(e^{j\omega})| = 1\) for all \(\omega\) — it passes all frequencies at equal amplitude but introduces a frequency-dependent phase shift, making it useful for phase equalization without affecting the magnitude response.

Key Takeaways

  • IIR filters can achieve the same frequency selectivity as an FIR filter with far fewer operations — but at the cost of nonlinear phase and potential instability
  • Always implement high-order IIR filters as cascades of biquads, never as a single high-order section — the cascade form is numerically stable
  • A pole close to the unit circle at angle θ creates a peak in the magnitude response near frequency θ rad/sample
  • Graphical method for frequency response: magnitude is the product of distances from zeros divided by the product of distances from poles, evaluated at e^jω

Applications

IIR filters are used in Lab 3 alongside FIR filters. A DC notch filter (pole near z=1 with a zero at z=1) removes DC offset — common in audio processing. Equalizers in audio systems are typically IIR biquad banks. The guitar effects in Lab 7 also exploit IIR feedback structures to create reverb and delay effects.

Topic 5 Sampling and Aliasing
›

Why This Matters

Aliasing is one of the most insidious failure modes in signal processing because it is silent and irreversible — the samples look like valid numbers, but they represent the wrong frequencies. This has caused real failures: audio systems that produce phantom tones, medical devices that misread sensor data, and radar systems that report false targets. The Nyquist theorem is not just theory — it is an engineering constraint that shapes every ADC interface design you will ever work on. Knowing when and why aliasing occurs is the first step to preventing it.

Concepts

Sampling is the bridge between the analog physical world and the digital DSP system. The Nyquist-Shannon Sampling Theorem establishes the fundamental requirement for perfect reconstruction: you must sample faster than twice the signal's one-sided bandwidth \(B\):

\[ f_s > 2B \]

When this condition is met, the discrete-time sequence contains all the information of the original continuous-time signal and it can be perfectly reconstructed by a lowpass filter. When it is violated, aliasing occurs — a catastrophic and irreversible form of distortion. A continuous-time sinusoid at frequency \(f_0\) sampled at rate \(f_s\) folds to a discrete-time frequency of:

\[ \omega_{alias} = \frac{2\pi\,(f_0 \bmod f_s)}{f_s} \]

The devastating consequence is that the aliased component is mathematically identical to a genuine lower-frequency sinusoid — there is no way to distinguish them after sampling. This is why an anti-aliasing lowpass filter must be applied to the analog signal before the ADC, removing any content above \(f_s/2\) before it can fold back into the signal band.

Aliasing is not just a mathematical curiosity — it shows up visually in everyday life. The wagon-wheel effect in movies, the standing-water illusion in slow-motion videos of flowing streams, and rolling shutter distortion in phone cameras are all direct spatial or temporal analogs of audio aliasing — the same math, different domain. Standard audio sampling rates reflect the bandwidth-complexity tradeoff: telephone audio at 8 kHz captures speech up to 4 kHz with a simple anti-aliasing filter; CD audio at 44.1 kHz covers the full human hearing range; studio audio at 192 kHz provides extra headroom to allow a gentler, lower-order anti-aliasing filter rolloff.

One advanced technique is bandpass sampling: deliberately sampling a bandpass signal centered at \(f_c\) at a rate much less than \(2f_c\), exploiting controlled aliasing to translate the signal down to baseband automatically. This is the principle behind software-defined radio receivers, where a single wideband ADC can replace an entire analog downconversion chain.

Key Takeaways

  • Aliasing is irreversible — once a signal is sampled below the Nyquist rate, the aliased frequencies cannot be separated from the original
  • Visual aliasing (wagon-wheel effect, water flowing demo, rolling shutter) directly illustrate the same phenomenon as audio aliasing
  • Sampling at exactly twice the signal frequency (edge case) yields a result that depends on the phase of the cosine — not guaranteed to be meaningful
  • Increasing sampling rate increases run-time complexity and the required filter order linearly

Applications

Every ADC and DAC in the lab hardware relies on these principles. The STM32's codec samples audio at 48 kHz with an integrated anti-aliasing filter. The data scrambler in Lab 4 generates pseudo-random binary sequences at a symbol rate tied directly to the sampling rate. Bandpass sampling is exploited in software-defined radio (SDR) receivers.

Topic 6 Interpolation and Pulse Shaping
›

Why This Matters

Every DAC output stage, every image scaling algorithm, every audio sample rate converter uses interpolation. When you AirPlay music to a speaker at a different sample rate than your phone, a polyphase interpolation filter converts between rates in real time. The raised cosine pulse introduced here is the most important waveform in digital communications — used in virtually every modem from dial-up to LTE. Without it, symbols collide with each other at the receiver and the communication link fails entirely.

Concepts

Interpolation is the reverse of sampling — it reconstructs a higher-rate or continuous-time signal from a lower-rate discrete sequence. In the context of digital communications, it is also the mechanism for pulse shaping: controlling the shape of the transmitted waveform to manage bandwidth and minimize inter-symbol interference.

The process of upsampling by L begins by inserting \(L-1\) zeros between every original sample:

\[ x_u[n] = \begin{cases} x[n/L] & \text{if } L \mid n \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases} \]

This zero-insertion stretches the spectrum in frequency, creating \(L-1\) spectral images (copies of the original spectrum) at multiples of \(2\pi/L\). To remove these images and recover a smooth, bandwidth-limited signal, a lowpass filter with cutoff \(\pi/L\) is applied immediately after upsampling. The choice of this lowpass filter — its impulse response — is the pulse shape.

The ideal reconstruction filter has a rectangular frequency response and a sinc impulse response, but the sinc is infinitely long. In practice, three pulse shapes are common: the rectangular pulse (zero-order hold — just hold each sample until the next one, like a DAC staircase), the triangular pulse (linear interpolation between samples), and the truncated sinc (windowed ideal lowpass). Each is a tradeoff between reconstruction quality and computational complexity.

For digital communications specifically, the standard pulse shape is the raised cosine, parameterized by the roll-off factor \(\alpha \in [0,1]\). It satisfies the Nyquist ISI criterion — its value is exactly zero at all nonzero integer multiples of the symbol period — which means symbols do not interfere with each other at the sampling instant. A smaller \(\alpha\) gives tighter bandwidth but requires a longer filter and is more sensitive to timing errors; a larger \(\alpha\) is more tolerant but uses more bandwidth. The polyphase filter bank provides a computationally efficient way to implement this upsampling-plus-filtering operation by avoiding the wasted computation on the inserted zero samples.

Key Takeaways

  • The ideal reconstruction filter is a sinc pulse — infinite in extent and non-causal, so it must be truncated and windowed for practical use
  • The raised cosine pulse satisfies the Nyquist ISI criterion: zero inter-symbol interference at sampling instants
  • Upsampling followed by pulse shaping is the standard transmitter chain in PAM/QAM communication systems

Applications

Pulse shaping is central to Labs 5 and 6. The baseband PAM transmitter upsamples the symbol sequence and passes it through a raised cosine filter to control the transmission bandwidth while eliminating ISI. The same chain is extended to complex-valued QAM signals in Lab 6.

Topic 7 Digital Pulse Amplitude Modulation (PAM)
›

Why This Matters

Every form of digital communication — USB, Ethernet, Wi-Fi, LTE, Bluetooth — uses some variant of pulse amplitude modulation at its physical layer. When you plug in a USB cable, PAM-3 encoding transmits data at 5 Gbps on that wire. When a dial-up modem connects over a phone line, it uses PAM at the acoustic level. The principles here are the foundation of the infrastructure the modern world depends on, and Lab 5 makes the full chain viscerally concrete.

Concepts

Pulse Amplitude Modulation (PAM) is the foundational scheme of digital communication. The idea is simple: map groups of bits to discrete amplitude levels and transmit a pulse of that amplitude once per symbol period. With \(M\) amplitude levels, each symbol carries \(J = \log_2 M\) bits, and the bit rate relates to the symbol rate by:

\[ R_b = J \cdot f_{sym}, \quad J = \log_2 M \quad \text{(bits/symbol)} \]

The baseband PAM transmitter chain works as follows. Bits arrive in groups of \(J\) and are mapped to symbol amplitudes — for 2-PAM: \(\pm 1\); for 4-PAM: \(\pm 1, \pm 3\). The symbol sequence is then upsampled by factor \(L\) (inserting \(L-1\) zeros between symbols) and passed through a pulse shaping filter — typically a raised cosine FIR — which simultaneously controls the transmitted bandwidth and prevents inter-symbol interference at the receiver's sampling instant.

At the baseband PAM receiver, the incoming waveform passes through a matched filter (the time-reversed pulse shape), is downsampled at the correct timing instant, and then the estimated amplitude is quantized to the nearest constellation point by a decision device. That decision is then demapped back to bits. Every step in this chain is a direct application of material from earlier lectures — sampling, FIR filtering, matched filtering, and quantization all appear explicitly.

A fundamental limit on how fast any channel can carry data is given by Shannon's channel capacity:

\[ C = B\log_2(1 + \mathrm{SNR}) \quad \text{bits/s} \]

This is a hard theoretical ceiling — no coding or modulation scheme, no matter how clever, can reliably communicate above this rate. The tradeoffs in PAM system design are direct: increasing \(M\) (more bits per symbol) improves spectral efficiency \(R_b/B\), but requires higher SNR to maintain the same error rate because the amplitude levels are closer together. Reducing the symbol rate uses less bandwidth but sacrifices throughput. Every real system (dial-up modem, LTE, Wi-Fi) navigates these tradeoffs explicitly.

Key Takeaways

  • More bits per symbol (larger M) increases spectral efficiency but requires higher SNR to maintain the same error rate
  • The pulse shaping filter at the transmitter and the matched filter at the receiver together minimize ISI and maximize SNR at the sampling instant
  • Real communication systems (dial-up modems, LTE, Wi-Fi) all implement variants of this PAM/QAM framework

Applications

Lab 5 implements a complete baseband PAM transceiver on the STM32. The transmitter and receiver run simultaneously with the channel between them. This is the most complex lab in the course and directly demonstrates the end-to-end communication system design flow.

Topic 8 Channel Impairments
›

Why This Matters

No real channel is ideal. Wireless signals bounce off buildings and arrive from multiple paths, each with a different delay and amplitude. This multipath is why Wi-Fi slows in crowded rooms, why phone calls drop in parking garages, and why 5G uses massive antenna arrays to steer beams around obstacles. Understanding how channels corrupt signals mathematically is what makes it possible to design receivers that compensate — the entire field of channel estimation and equalization flows from this topic.

Concepts

No real communication channel transmits a signal perfectly — every channel introduces some combination of noise, distortion, and interference. Understanding these channel impairments is essential before we can design receivers that compensate for them.

The most universal impairment is additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) — thermal noise generated by the random motion of electrons in every conductor and resistor. It is modeled as a random signal added to the transmitted waveform, with a flat power spectral density of \(N_0/2\) W/Hz. "White" means its power is equal at all frequencies, and "Gaussian" means its amplitude distribution follows a normal distribution. This model underlies the BER derivations in the matched filtering topic.

A second critical impairment is multipath propagation: in wireless environments, the transmitted signal arrives at the receiver via multiple paths — direct line-of-sight, plus reflections off buildings, walls, and the ground. Each path has its own delay and attenuation, so the received signal is a weighted sum of delayed copies of the transmitted signal. This is mathematically a convolution with the channel impulse response, making the channel itself an FIR filter. When this channel memory spans multiple symbol periods, it causes inter-symbol interference (ISI) — energy from one symbol leaks into adjacent symbol intervals, corrupting the decision at the receiver. The key performance metric in this environment is SINR (signal-to-interference-plus-noise ratio), which accounts for both the noise floor and the interference from neighboring symbols.

Additional impairments include phase jitter — slow random variations in the carrier phase caused by oscillator instability — and narrowband interference, such as the 2.4 GHz interference a microwave oven causes to nearby Wi-Fi. The latter can be suppressed with a notch filter, while multipath is addressed through channel estimation followed by equalization. Channel estimation works by transmitting a known training sequence (such as the PRBS from Lab 4), comparing the received version to the known transmitted version, and solving for the channel impulse response that maps one to the other.

Key Takeaways

  • Reverb in a room is physically the same phenomenon as wireless multipath — both are a sum of delayed, attenuated copies of the source
  • Microwave oven interference on 2.4 GHz Wi-Fi is a textbook example of narrowband interference suppression by notch filtering
  • 5G mmWave beam steering is needed to combat the high path loss at millimeter-wave frequencies — physics drives the system design

Applications

Lab 5 introduces a simulated multipath channel between the PAM transmitter and receiver. Lab 7 deliberately creates reverb and echo effects using FIR and IIR delay networks — turning a channel impairment into a creative audio tool. Channel estimation is used in Lab 6 for QAM synchronization.

Topic 9 Matched Filtering
›

Why This Matters

The matched filter is one of the most powerful results in signal processing: a single elegant derivation gives you the exact receiver filter that maximizes SNR and minimizes bit errors. It is not abstract — GPS receivers use matched filtering to detect signals 20 dB below the noise floor. Radar uses it to extract target reflections from clutter. MRI uses it in pulse sequence design. Every time your phone receives a text in a weak signal area, a matched filter is part of why it worked.

Concepts

Given that the channel adds AWGN to our transmitted signal, a natural question arises: what is the optimal way to filter the received signal before making a symbol decision? The answer is the matched filter — the linear filter that maximizes the signal-to-noise ratio at the decision sampling instant.

The derivation proceeds by expressing the output SNR as a function of the filter's frequency response and applying the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality to find the maximizer. The result is elegant: the optimal filter's impulse response is the time-reversed, conjugated version of the transmit pulse shape \(p[n]\):

\[ h_{MF}[n] = p^*[N - n] \]

This makes intuitive sense — the matched filter is "tuned" to the shape of the signal it is trying to detect. Its output is the cross-correlation of the received signal with the pulse shape, which peaks precisely when the received pulse is aligned with the filter. When the transmitted and received pulses are the same (no channel distortion), this output equals the autocorrelation of the pulse shape:

\[ y[n] = (p * h_{MF})[n] = \sum_k p[k]\,p[k-n] \]

After the matched filter output is sampled at the correct timing instant, the estimated symbol amplitude is compared to decision thresholds. In the presence of AWGN only (no ISI), the probability of a bit error for 2-PAM is:

\[ P_b = Q\!\left(\sqrt{\frac{2E_b}{N_0}}\right), \quad Q(x) = \frac{1}{2}\,\mathrm{erfc}\!\left(\frac{x}{\sqrt{2}}\right) \]

where \(E_b/N_0\) is the energy per bit to noise spectral density ratio. This curve decreases steeply with SNR — a 3 dB increase in \(E_b/N_0\) roughly halves the BER in the steep part of the curve. The eye diagram provides a visual diagnostic: by overlaying many symbol periods of the matched filter output on a single display, the "opening" of the eye indicates how much noise margin exists. A wide-open eye means low ISI and easy decisions; a partially closed eye signals ISI or timing errors. Gray coding minimizes the BER impact of symbol errors by assigning bit patterns to amplitude levels so adjacent levels differ by only one bit — a symbol error (to the nearest neighbor) then causes just one bit error rather than multiple.

Key Takeaways

  • The matched filter is the receiver counterpart to the transmitter's pulse shaping filter — together they form a Nyquist pair that eliminates ISI and maximizes SNR
  • Gray coding halves the BER compared to natural binary coding at the same symbol error rate — it costs nothing computationally
  • Adding a pause (guard interval) between transmitted symbols reduces ISI at the cost of reduced spectral efficiency — a fundamental tradeoff seen again in OFDM

Applications

The matched filter is implemented in Lab 5 at the PAM receiver. The steepest descent algorithm introduced here is the foundation for LMS adaptive equalizers used in modems and wireless receivers. FFT-based multicarrier communication (Wi-Fi, LTE, cable modems) uses matched filtering at each subcarrier.

The probability that a received bit is decoded incorrectly; decreases as Eb/N₀ increases
Eb/N₀
Energy per bit to noise spectral density ratio — the fundamental SNR measure for digital communication systems

A bit assignment where adjacent constellation points differ by exactly one bit — minimizes BER for a given symbol error rate

Topic 10 Quadrature Amplitude Modulation (QAM)
›

Why This Matters

Wi-Fi 6 uses 1024-QAM. 5G NR uses 256-QAM. Cable internet uses 4096-QAM. Every bit per second of modern high-speed wireless communication rides on the principle introduced here: two independent data streams can share the same bandwidth and frequency, separated only by the mathematical orthogonality of cosine and sine. QAM is not a clever trick — it is the reason mobile broadband exists at the speeds it does. Lab 6 makes the challenge of carrier phase synchronization viscerally real: a few degrees of error corrupts both channels simultaneously.

Concepts

Quadrature Amplitude Modulation (QAM) is the natural extension of PAM into two dimensions. The key insight is that a cosine and a sine at the same frequency are orthogonal — they do not interfere with each other when multiplied and integrated over a symbol period. This orthogonality allows us to transmit two completely independent PAM streams simultaneously in the same bandwidth, effectively doubling spectral efficiency at no additional cost in bandwidth.

The QAM transmitter generates an in-phase (I) stream and a quadrature (Q) stream independently: each goes through symbol mapping, upsampling, and pulse shaping just like a PAM transmitter. The two streams are then modulated onto cosine and sine carriers at the same carrier frequency \(f_c\) and summed: \(s(t) = I(t)\cos(2\pi f_c t) - Q(t)\sin(2\pi f_c t)\). At the QAM receiver, the received signal is separately multiplied by \(\cos(2\pi f_c t)\) and \(-\sin(2\pi f_c t)\), lowpass filtered, matched filtered, and downsampled. If the carrier phase is perfectly synchronized, the I and Q streams are perfectly separated.

The set of all possible transmitted symbols forms a constellation — a 2-D grid of points in the I-Q plane. 4-QAM (also called QPSK) has four points arranged in a square. 16-QAM has 16 points in a 4×4 grid, carrying 4 bits per symbol. 64-QAM carries 6 bits per symbol. Each step up in constellation order doubles the number of symbols and adds 1 bit per symbol, but requires the noise margin between adjacent points to shrink — demanding higher SNR to maintain the same error rate.

The critical practical challenge in QAM is phase synchronization. Even a small phase offset between transmitter and receiver carrier oscillators rotates the entire constellation — I and Q components mix into each other, a condition called I/Q crosstalk, which catastrophically degrades decision accuracy. Real systems use pilot symbols, Costas loops, or decision-directed algorithms to continuously estimate and correct the carrier phase. Gray coding is applied in both the I and Q dimensions independently so that adjacent constellation points differ by only one bit, minimizing the bit error impact of symbol errors to the nearest neighbor.

Key Takeaways

  • QAM achieves higher spectral efficiency than PAM by exploiting the orthogonality of cosine and sine at the same frequency
  • Every modern wireless standard (Wi-Fi, LTE, 5G) uses QAM — typically 64-QAM or 256-QAM in good channel conditions, falling back to QPSK in poor conditions
  • Phase and frequency synchronization between transmitter and receiver are critical — small phase offsets rotate the constellation and cause decision errors

Applications

Lab 6 implements a complete baseband QAM transceiver on the STM32. The STM32 simultaneously runs the I and Q transmit chains and the receiver chains, using the audio codec as the channel. This is the most communication-theory-dense lab in the course.

Topic 11 Quantization
›

Why This Matters

The CD standard uses 16-bit quantization — chosen because 96 dB of dynamic range exceeds the practical limit of human hearing. Medical ultrasound uses 12-bit ADCs because the hardware runs at hundreds of MHz. Your phone's microphone uses a delta-sigma ADC with noise shaping to achieve 24-bit effective resolution at audio rates. Every bit-depth choice is a quantization tradeoff. Getting it wrong means wasted silicon (too many bits) or audible noise and measurement error (too few) — the 6 dB/bit rule makes this tradeoff precise and quantifiable.

Concepts

Quantization is the amplitude analog of sampling: just as sampling discretizes a signal in time, quantization discretizes it in amplitude. Every ADC performs both operations simultaneously — it samples the signal and quantizes the result to a finite number of bits. Understanding quantization is essential for managing the tradeoff between precision and speed in embedded DSP systems.

A uniform mid-rise quantizer with \(B\) bits divides the input range into \(2^B\) equal levels. The spacing between adjacent levels is the step size:

\[ \Delta = \frac{x_{\max} - x_{\min}}{2^B} \]

Any input value that falls between two levels is rounded to the nearest one, introducing a quantization error bounded by \(\pm \Delta/2\). When the input signal is smooth relative to \(\Delta\), this error is well modeled as additive white noise uniformly distributed over \([-\Delta/2, \Delta/2]\), giving a quantization noise power of:

\[ \sigma_q^2 = \frac{\Delta^2}{12} \]

For a full-scale sinusoidal input, the signal power relative to this noise power gives the signal-to-quantization-noise ratio (SQNR). Substituting and simplifying yields the remarkably clean rule of thumb:

\[ \mathrm{SQNR} \approx 6.02B + 1.76 \text{ dB} \]

Every additional bit of resolution adds approximately 6 dB of dynamic range. This is why audio CDs use 16-bit quantization (~98 dB dynamic range) while professional studio equipment uses 24 bits (~146 dB). A key engineering tradeoff is that ADC speed and precision are in fundamental tension: a 12.5 GSPS digitizer for radar may have only 8 bits of resolution (~50 dB SQNR), while a precision measurement converter at 500 kSPS achieves 24 bits. Noise shaping (used in delta-sigma ADCs) offers a way to partially escape this tradeoff — by using feedback to push quantization noise out of the signal band and into higher frequencies where it can be removed by a lowpass filter, effectively trading bandwidth for amplitude precision.

Key Takeaways

  • Every additional bit of ADC resolution gives approximately 6 dB of SQNR — a clean rule of thumb for system design
  • ADC precision and sampling rate are in tension: high-speed digitizers (e.g., 12.5 GSPS) typically have only 8 bits, while high-precision converters (24 bits) run slowly (500 kSPS)
  • Fixed-point arithmetic in embedded DSP must account for quantization error accumulation across filter taps

Applications

The STM32H735's audio codec uses 24-bit ADC/DAC at 48 kHz. The guitar effects in Lab 7 exploit controlled quantization (bitcrushing) as a creative effect alongside reverb and delay. Understanding ADC/DAC tradeoffs is essential for any embedded measurement or communication system design.

Vocabulary

Quantization
Mapping continuous amplitude values to a finite set of discrete levels — the amplitude analog of sampling in time
SQNR
Signal-to-Quantization-Noise Ratio — increases by ~6 dB for each additional bit of ADC resolution
Step Size (Δ)
The spacing between adjacent quantization levels; smaller Δ means finer resolution and lower quantization noise
Fixed-Point Arithmetic
Arithmetic using integers with a fixed binary point — faster than floating-point on embedded processors but subject to overflow and rounding
Noise Shaping
A technique that moves quantization noise energy from the signal band to out-of-band frequencies using feedback — used in delta-sigma ADCs

The ratio of the largest to smallest representable signal amplitude; approximately 6·B dB for a B-bit uniform quantizer

Overview
